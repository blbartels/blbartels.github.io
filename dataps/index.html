Preface

This book is primarily targeted toward undergraduate students taking a course in data analysis and statistics. Many statistics and/or data science books are geared toward general audiences, statistics students, economics students, or students in other disciplines. Those books typically do not contain many political science examples. This book is designed to give students a foundation in data analysis and statistics but with a political science focus. The use of examples helps solidify complicated concepts surrounding data analysis and statistics. Political science students can relate to these political science examples and are ideally more motivated to learn data analysis and statistics related to political science and government topics.

The book places a focus on answering substantive political science questions using data analysis and statistics. The book uses RStudio (which runs R; see Chapter 1) as the statistical computing software package. Chapter 1 includes basic details on installing and using RStudio as well as a program within RStudio called Quarto, which generates documents (.pdf or .html) integrating data analysis results and writing and interpretation.

Chapter 2 first covers the basics of the social science research process including posing interesting research questions, defining concepts, elucidating empirically-oriented theory and hypotheses, and testing those hypotheses with data and methods. Data and methods are means, while answering substantive research questions and testing hypotheses are the ends in substantively oriented political science research. The chapter also discusses issues causality and causal inference; how do analysts propose research designs that can isolate cause and effect? Chapter 2 then introduces readers to the foundations of understanding “data”: Understanding variables (as empirical manifestations of concepts), units of analysis, how variables are measured, and the difference between a dependent variable and independent variable.

Chapter 3 then introduces students to cleaning data, recoding variables, merging datasets together, and using other data organization tools to simplify and streamline data analysis. These are critical tools that serve as foundations for all subsequent chapters.

Chapter 4 moves to a core data analysis topic of data visualization, which has become particularly important in data science. This chapter introduces students to univariate (one variable) summaries of variables in their data. Univariate graphs — histograms, bar graphs, boxplots, and density plots — allow analysts to communicate the nature of central tendency (the most representative value of a variable) and dispersion of a variable (how spread out are the values of the variable). On the latter, is the distribution of a variable unimodal and bell-shaped? Is the distribution symmetric? Or is it bimodal or skewed? The way a variable is measured (as, e.g., continuous or categorical) also dictates how one communicate conclusions about central tendency and dispersion. The chapter also introduces students to the powerful and popular graphing package ggplot2, which is under the umbrella of the popular and important tidyverse suite of packages.

Chapter 5 confronts another core data visualization topic: Geographic maps that display how a variable is distributed across geographic units (e.g., states, countries, counties, congressional districts, etc.). These maps are called “choropleth maps.” The chapter introduces students to creating choropleth maps, envisioning what information the analyst wants to communicate via palettes and other important map features, and interpreting the results to make substantive conclusions about political phenomena.

The next three chapters cover core statistical topics that are geared toward a political science audience. Chapter 6 covers descriptive statistics and is directly connected to Chapter 4 on data visualization. Chapter 6 first covers univariate statistical measures of central tendency and dispersion with a focus on when to use what types of measures (e.g., depending on the level of measurement the variable takes on). The chapter also discusses other descriptions of variables, including frequency distributions (for categorical variables). Chapter 6 then covers bivariate comparisons (associations between two variables) using correlations and cross-tabulations (or “cross-tabs”).

Chapter 7 covers inferential statistics, which is usually when students’ eyes begin to glaze over. I try to present this material in a disarming manner, lifting the veil of complexity and explaining complicated concepts the clearest terms possible. Inferential statistics centers on making inferences from sample to population. To make a statistical inference is to draw a conclusion about “unknown facts” (quantities in the population we can never know) based on “known facts” (quantities in a sample that we do know). Classic theory on probability and distributions gives us a framework for making such inferences. The chapter confronts the related topic of hypothesis testing using the scientific method, including drawing conclusions about whether the effect of an independent variable on a dependent variable is “statistically significant.”

Chapter 8 brings many of the prior chapters together with linear regression analysis. The prior chapters addressed associations between variables and sometimes (but not always) specified the direction of “causality” from independent variable to dependent variable. Linear regression analysis explicitly evaluates these types of relationship in either simple regression (examining the effect of just one independent variable on a dependent variable) and multiple regression (examining the effects of multiple independent variables on a dependent variable). The chapter introduces how multiple regression estimates an effect of an independent variable while controlling for other independent variables (e.g., controlling for “alternative causes”). The chapter emphasizes communicating the direction of these effects, their size, and also statistical significance, which relates to hypothesis testing.

Finally, Chapter 9 discusses strategies for “bringing it all together,” that is, summarizing one’s results from an empirical analysis employing many of the methods discussed herein to answer political science research questions and test hypotheses.
